Use Case Development & Auto-ML Problem Framing

April 5, 2026

The hardest part of applied ML isn't building the model — it's knowing what to build. Most practitioners start with data and ask "what can I predict?" I had to work the other direction: start with a business domain and ask "what does this organization actually need to optimize?" That discipline — translating a domain problem into a tractable, schema-driven ML problem — is one I've applied across three very different verticals on a production AI platform.

My process was consistent across all three: define the business objective first, then identify the KPIs that would tell you whether you achieved it, then design a data schema — organized into logical data products and elements — that the dataset needed to satisfy. The ML problem type — time series, classification, regression — was always an output of this analysis, never an assumption going in.

Sales Forecasting — Consumer Electronics

I designed this use case around inventory optimization: predict unit sales with enough accuracy to reduce both stockouts and overstock simultaneously. The schema covered everything that drives demand in consumer electronics: sales history, pricing and margin dynamics, inventory levels, promotional spend, regional performance, and seasonal patterns.

After schema design and auto-ML model selection, the best-performing model achieved a mean absolute percentage error of about 16%. The resulting demand forecast, applied to current inventory gaps, projected a 36% increase in monthly units sold and a $64M revenue uplift from inventory optimization. This use case was templatized and pushed to production.

Demand Forecasting — Pharmaceutical Supply Chain

Pharmaceutical supply chains carry stakes that consumer goods don't — a stockout isn't just lost revenue, it can mean a patient doesn't get their medication. I framed the objective around minimizing Days Inventory Outstanding while protecting service levels, and defined KPIs that supply chain teams actually track: On-Time In-Full delivery rate, DIO, fill rate, and working capital tied up in inventory.

The schema required understanding pharmaceutical distribution concepts: warehouse transfers, safety stock calculations, expiry tracking, demand variability by SKU, and reorder policies. This wasn't generic — I had to learn enough about the domain to know which data elements actually drove the outcomes the business cared about.

The best model achieved a mean absolute percentage error under 6%. The analysis projected On-Time In-Full improvement from 87% to a forecasted 97%, DIO reduction from 82 days to a projected 45 days, and a €40M reduction in working capital — with €15–20M in inventory-driven savings identified as immediately actionable. Both this use case and the sales forecasting one were presented directly to founders.

Loan Fraud & Risk Detection — Fintech

Fraud detection is structurally different from forecasting. You're not predicting a number — you're making a binary decision (approve or flag), and the two ways you can be wrong are not equally costly. Miss a fraudulent application and the lender absorbs the full loan loss. Flag a legitimate customer and you've lost their business and trust.

That asymmetry shaped how I framed the problem. The goal wasn't high accuracy overall — it was catching as many fraudulent cases as possible without unnecessarily rejecting good applicants. The schema I designed captured the five dimensions that determine lending risk: who the applicant is, what they're applying for, how they've repaid debts in the past, how they currently use credit, and derived risk signals like debt-to-income ratio and bureau score tier.

When the dataset ran through the pipeline, the classification problem type was identified automatically from the binary target variable. The correlation analysis surfaced which features actually separated good and bad loans. The segmentation step revealed that the borrower population wasn't uniform — different risk profiles clustered in ways that weren't visible at the aggregate level, which is exactly the kind of insight that helps underwriters make better decisions at the individual case level. High-income applicants with thin credit files behaved differently from middle-income applicants with established but damaged histories — two distinct risk profiles requiring different underwriting approaches, surfaced automatically.

The Through-Line

What these three use cases share isn't domain knowledge — it's the practice of defining what "success" looks like before touching the data. That definition shaped everything downstream: which features mattered, which correlations were surfaced, which segments were extracted, and what the model was actually being asked to optimize. The platform handled the ML execution. My job was to make sure it was solving the right problem.

The ML problem type was always an output of the domain analysis, never a starting assumption. Forecasting became time series because the business cared about future values over time. Fraud became classification because the decision was binary and the cost asymmetry demanded a decision threshold. Knowing which of those you're solving changes everything about how you design the schema, select features, and evaluate results. Starting from the business question rather than the technique is what made the platform useful across verticals.
